# Inherited Rule Audit

## Scope

This Stage 2A table audits the major rule categories inherited from Stop Slop for possible relevance to postgraduate academic writing in education, TESOL, and the social sciences. The source files are design inputs, not validated evidence. `Source file` identifies the original Stop Slop material from which a rule was inherited or derived. `references/do_not_overcorrect.md` is a project guardrail, not an inherited source rule. Classifications are provisional and do not authorise automatic correction or inference about authorship.

| GEN-PHR-001 | Delete all throat-clearing openers and state the point directly. | `references/phrases.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Some openers are empty, but scope-setting and problem framing can orient readers. | Flag a defined opener only when removable without loss of stance, scope, or cohesion; check repetition. | Purpose statements, definitions, quoted language, deliberate framing. | Introduction; Discussion; general. | Rationale plausible; corpus and reviewer testing needed. |
| GEN-PHR-002 | Delete emphasis crutches such as standalone commands or generic importance signals. | `references/phrases.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Generic emphasis may be unsupported, but causal explanation may legitimately state why a result matters. | Flag performative or semantically empty emphasis; request the specific consequence or evidence. | Explicit contribution statements, pedagogic genres, quoted material. | Introduction; Discussion; general. | Expert review and false-positive examples needed. |

| GEN-PHR-006 | Remove all self-referential metacommentary and section previews. | `references/phrases.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Academic metadiscourse can guide readers through long arguments and meet genre expectations. | Flag informal or redundant announcements; retain concise, accurate signposting where useful. | Thesis roadmaps, section previews, method orientation, reflective genres. | Introduction; chapter openings; Methodology. | Academic metadiscourse literature and reviewer input needed. |

| GEN-PHR-009 | Delete vague declaratives that announce importance without specifics. | `references/phrases.md` | Inherited | Retain | Automatic deletion could remove a topic sentence whose support follows. | Flag when neither the sentence nor nearby context specifies the consequence, scope, or evidence. | Topic sentences followed by explicit elaboration; concise abstracts. | Introduction; Discussion; Abstract. | Local-context window requires evaluation. |
| GEN-STR-001 | Remove binary contrasts and state the positive claim directly. | `references/structures.md`; `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Contrast and qualification are central to academic argument; deleting negation can misrepresent alternatives. | Flag repeated theatrical reversals, not contrast itself; preserve analytically necessary distinctions. | Hypothesis comparison, misconception correction, theoretical distinctions, “not only ... but also.” | Literature Review; Discussion; Introduction. | Functional and repetition criteria need evidence. |
| GEN-STR-002 | Remove negative lists that delay the positive claim. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Negation may delimit a construct or reject plausible alternatives. | Flag extended dramatic buildup; retain concise exclusions that perform analytical work. | Definitions by exclusion, differential explanation, hypothesis rejection. | Literature Review; Discussion; Findings. | Provisional; expert review needed. |
| GEN-STR-003 | Replace dramatic sentence fragments with complete sentences. | `references/structures.md`; `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Fragments can be intentional in headings, quotations, or qualitative presentation. | Flag unexplained fragments in expository prose, especially repeated emphasis fragments. | Headings, labels, transcripts, quotations, creative/reflective assignments. | General; Discussion. | Trigger definition, genre review, and local evaluation required. |
| GEN-STR-004 | Avoid rhetorical setups and rhetorical questions; state the answer directly. | `references/structures.md`; `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Questions can frame genuine inquiry or organise argument; blanket removal may erase research purpose. | Flag questions that presume agreement, manufacture suspense, or substitute for a claim; protect real research questions. | Research questions, pedagogic/reflexive genres, conceptual problem framing, quotations. | Introduction; Discussion; dedicated research-question sections. | Function classification and reviewer evidence needed. |
| GEN-STR-005 | Avoid listed narrative templates and indirect “X that is not Y” constructions. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Needs evidence | The templates may be rare or harmless in academic genres; indirectness can encode necessary restriction. | Do not implement pending corpus frequency, function, and false-positive analysis. | Narrative inquiry, reflective accounts, restrictive definitions. | Introduction; Methodology; reflective assignments. | No academic evidence established. |
| GEN-STR-006 | Give every sentence a human actor; treat inanimate subjects as false agency. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Reject | Forces inaccurate agents and conflicts with conventional theory-, study-, institution-, process-, and result-centred prose. | Exclude the absolute; review only unsupported intentional/causal agency or hidden accountability. | Conventional metonymy, models predicting, studies examining, processes producing effects. | All. | Narrow semantic rule requires expert and corpus validation. |
| GEN-STR-007 | Treat “the data tell us” and related data-centred verbs as false agency. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Adapt | “Data suggest/indicate/show” can mark conventional, calibrated evidential stance. | Permit calibrated evidential verbs; flag anthropomorphic or overstrong claims and interpretations exceeding analysis. | Discipline-accepted result statements and cautious inference. | Findings; Discussion. | Reporting-verb hierarchy needs literature and reviewer support. |
| GEN-STR-008 | Replace distant narration with second-person “you” when no actor is available. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Reject | May create informality, impose experience on the reader, or violate institutional conventions. | Never introduce “you” by default; choose a supported actor, accurate impersonal form, or legitimate abstract subject. | Reflective assignments, instructions, practitioner guidance, permitted pedagogic address. | Generally excluded from conventional research sections. | Genre-specific permissions need documentation. |
| GEN-STR-009 | Ban passive voice and put the actor first in every sentence. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Passive voice can appropriately foreground method, phenomenon, or result; forced revision may invent responsibility. | Flag only when a consequential agent is obscured or repeated passives impair comprehension. | Reproducible procedures, agent-irrelevant processes, result foregrounding, established knowledge. | Methodology; Findings; all where accountability matters. | Academic writing literature review and local evaluation required. |
| GEN-STR-010 | Ban sentence starters beginning with What, When, Where, Which, Who, Why, or How. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Surface-form ban catches research questions, cohesive pseudo-clefts, definitions, and scope statements. | Review repeated/vague pseudo-clefts; never flag a Wh-opener solely by first word. | Explicit questions, definitions, temporal/spatial clauses, legitimate cohesive focus. | Introduction; Literature Review; Discussion. | Syntactic/function specification needs testing. |
| GEN-STR-011 | Do not begin paragraphs with “So” or sentences with “Look.” | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Register concern may be valid, but “so” can express a logical result and quotations must be preserved. | Flag informal discourse-marker use in formal exposition; suggest a relation-specific transition only if needed. | Quotations, transcripts, reflective writing, mathematical/logical “so.” | All. | Genre and local-style evidence needed. |
| GEN-STR-012 | Never use a three-item list; use one or two items. | `references/structures.md`; `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Reject | Lists often encode real categories, variables, stages, or findings; shortening deletes content. | Exclude count-based rule; review only repeated ornamental triads unsupported by analysis. | Exhaustive taxonomies, themes, research questions, constructs, procedures. | All; especially Methodology and Findings. | No basis for count ban; ornamental criterion needs evidence. |
| GEN-STR-013 | Avoid questions immediately answered by the writer. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Question-answer sequences can organise complex exposition or mirror a research programme. | Flag only repetitive, theatrical, or unnecessary self-answering; assess whether a declarative improves clarity. | Explicit subquestions, pedagogic explanation, structured argument. | Introduction; Literature Review; Discussion. | Genre corpus and reviewer comparison needed. |
| GEN-STR-014 | Avoid ending every paragraph with a punchy statement. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Needs evidence | “Punchy” is subjective; concluding synthesis is often desirable. | Do not implement until cadence and legitimate synthesis can be distinguished reliably. | Topic synthesis, implications, chapter-level signposting. | Literature Review; Discussion; general. | Operational definition absent. |
| GEN-STR-015 | Remove every em dash and replace it with commas or periods. | `references/structures.md`; `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Mechanical replacement may damage syntax; style guides differ. | Review repeated or disruptive use; recommend punctuation based on syntax and required style guide. | Controlled parenthetical use, quotations, style-guide permission. | All. | Style-guide and institutional variation must be mapped. |
| GEN-STR-016 | Avoid stacked short, punchy, or staccato sentences. | `references/structures.md`; `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Short sentences can clarify dense reasoning, and qualitative excerpts may be fragmentary. | Flag clusters used chiefly for manufactured emphasis; assess cohesion and genre. | Deliberate clarification, headings, transcripts, quoted data. | General; Discussion. | Threshold and false-positive evaluation needed. |
| GEN-STR-017 | Avoid lazy extremes such as always, never, everyone, and nobody. | `references/structures.md` | Inherited | Retain | Universal language may overstate evidence, though it can be logically or empirically justified. | Flag unqualified universal claims for scope/evidence review; do not auto-replace with a hedge. | Logical definitions, complete-population data, accurate quotations. | All; especially Findings and Discussion. | Calibration against evidence and context needed. |
| GEN-STY-001 | Shorten sentences and remove runway wherever possible. | `references/examples.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Splitting or compression can change logical scope, citation attachment, qualification, and disciplinary tone. | Review high syntactic load or ambiguity for clarity, not minimum length. | GEN-STY-002 | Remove common academic phrases as formulaic or generic language. | `references/phrases.md` | Inherited | Adapt | Conventional lexical bundles support cohesion and genre participation; frequency is not evidence of poor quality or authorship. | Flag only defined, locally redundant, misleading, or excessively repeated phrases. Project guardrail: a common phrase is not by itself evidence of authorship or a reason to revise. | Signposting, cautious framing, definitions, discipline-specific bundles. | All, section-sensitive. | Corpus, multilingual-writer, and expert evidence required. |
| GEN-STY-003 | Remove all hedging and softening to make claims direct. | `references/phrases.md` | Inherited | Reject | Creates false certainty, broadens claims, and erases responsible scholarly stance. | Project guardrail: preserve evidence-calibrated hedging and never strengthen a claim without evidential justification. A narrower review may address stacked, contradictory, or functionless hedges. | Epistemic modality, approximators, probability, limitations, cautious reporting verbs. | All; especially Literature Review, Findings, Discussion. | Absolute rejected on academic-function grounds; narrow rule needs evaluation. |
