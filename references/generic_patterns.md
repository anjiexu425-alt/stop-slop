# Generic Patterns

## Purpose and status

This file summarises how Stage 2A treats generic patterns inherited from Stop Slop. The original files are source material only: they are not an academic style guide and must not be copied wholesale into an executable rule library. `references/do_not_overcorrect.md` supplies current project guardrails rather than inherited source rules. This summary is also not the final executable academic rule library. Detailed classifications, origins, exceptions, and evidence status are recorded in `docs/inherited_rule_audit.md`; design reasoning is recorded in `docs/rule_design_rationale.md`.

## Provisionally retained

The audit provisionally retains four rules whose original triggers and core treatments remain substantially unchanged: performative sincerity or false intimacy, unsupported announcements of significance or difficulty, vague importance claims, and unqualified universal claims (`GEN-PHR-007`–`GEN-PHR-009`, `GEN-STR-017`). Retention does not authorise automatic deletion or inference about authorship.

## Patterns requiring conditions

Potential throat-clearing, emphasis crutches, business jargon, filler, metadiscourse, binary contrasts, negative listings, fragments, rhetorical setups, passive voice, Wh-openers, em dashes, rhetorical questions, staccato clusters, sentence complexity, common academic phrases, and data-centred evidential expressions require context-dependent rules (`GEN-PHR-001`–`GEN-PHR-003`, `GEN-PHR-005`, `GEN-PHR-006`, `GEN-STR-001`–`GEN-STR-004`, `GEN-STR-007`, `GEN-STR-009`–`GEN-STR-011`, `GEN-STR-013`, `GEN-STR-015`, `GEN-STR-016`, `GEN-STY-001`, `GEN-STY-002`). `GEN-STR-002`, `GEN-STR-003`, and `GEN-STR-016` are adapted because academic exceptions materially change their trigger, scope, and action. Any later implementation must account for rhetorical function, section, discipline, repetition, evidence, and protected academic uses.

In particular, passive voice may be appropriate in Methodology; “the data suggest” may express calibrated inference; Wh-openers may frame a real research question; and conventional academic bundles may support cohesion. Form alone is insufficient for a correction.

## Patterns excluded in inherited form

The academic rule library should not inherit blanket deletion of adverbs or hedges, a requirement for a human actor in every sentence, default substitution of second-person “you,” or a ban on three-item lists (`GEN-PHR-004`, `GEN-STR-006`, `GEN-STR-008`, `GEN-STR-012`, `GEN-STY-003`). The absolute demand to shorten prose is also excluded; only a contextual clarity review is retained under `GEN-STY-001`.

These exclusions do not imply that intensifiers, vague agency, ornamental triads, or needlessly complex sentences can never merit review. Any narrower future rule must have its own rationale, safeguards, and evidence.

## Patterns awaiting evidence

Narrative templates, repeated punch-line endings, and the usefulness of pattern-density signals remain unresolved (`GEN-STR-005`, `GEN-STR-014`, `GEN-STY-004`). Pattern density is a research hypothesis derived from the inherited rules, not an explicit or validated Stop Slop rule. No implementation decision should be made until relevant literature, corpus evidence, expert review, and false-positive analysis support a defensible specification.

## Use in later stages

Later rule design should use the Rule IDs rather than reproducing long phrase blacklists. A future flag must describe a potentially unhelpful pattern, explain the possible issue, show reasonable exceptions, preserve meaning and claim strength, and allow the writer to keep the original. No textual feature described here can establish or infer authorship, and nothing here supports detection-evasion claims.
