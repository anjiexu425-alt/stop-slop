# Academic Structure Pilot Rules

## Scope and status

This reference contains the canonical formal definitions of pilot rules 5–6. The rules review textual structures rather than infer author identity. Both are unvalidated pilot heuristics. Rhetorical form alone is never sufficient evidence of a problem.

## Rule 5 — Theatrical binary contrast

| Field | Definition |
|---|---|
| **1. Rule ID** | `ACP-STR-001` |
| **2. Rule name** | Theatrical binary contrast |
| **3. Rule behaviour** | Flag |
| **4. Category** | Structure — contrast framing |
| **5. Unit of analysis** | Two adjacent sentence fragments or sentences, plus the surrounding paragraph |
| **6. Trigger** | Flag for review when repeated, emphatic “Not X. But Y.” or equivalent fragments stage a dramatic binary while suppressing a relevant relationship, qualification, or continuum. Require both theatrical form and an apparent oversimplification or repeated pattern. |
| **7. Academic risk** | Dramatic binary framing can overstate opposition, fragment reasoning, or replace an explanation of how two positions relate. |
| **8. Protected contexts** | Genuine theoretical distinctions; explicit hypothesis comparisons; correction of a documented misunderstanding; concise contrast where X and Y are mutually exclusive; quoted material; a single purposeful contrast that improves clarity. |
| **9. Applicable sections** | Literature Review, Findings, Discussion, and General Academic Writing. In Methodology, apply only when the contrast characterises methodological alternatives rather than clearly distinguishing procedural choices. |
| **10. Severity** | Medium |
| **11. User-facing explanation** | This contrast may present two ideas as a dramatic binary without explaining their relationship or necessary qualification. |
| **12. Suggested action** | Review whether the opposition is accurate and useful; if not, explain the relationship or combine the fragments. The user may keep the structure. |
| **13. Do-not-auto-rewrite constraint** | Do not merge or rewrite sentences automatically, remove a valid distinction, infer the author's intended relationship, or invent qualifications. |
| **14. Evidence status** | Unvalidated pilot heuristic. Pattern frequency, theatricality, and oversimplification require human annotation and genre-sensitive testing. |
| **15. Trigger-positive synthetic example** | **Synthetic example:** “This is not a minor qualification. It is a fundamental rejection of the entire approach.” |
| **16. Protected / false-positive synthetic example** | **Synthetic example:** “The distinction concerns reliability, not validity.” |

## Rule 6 — Formulaic rhetorical setup

| Field | Definition |
|---|---|
| **1. Rule ID** | `ACP-STR-002` |
| **2. Rule name** | Formulaic rhetorical setup |
| **3. Rule behaviour** | Flag |
| **4. Category** | Structure — rhetorical framing |
| **5. Unit of analysis** | Question and its following sentence or paragraph, interpreted within the section |
| **6. Trigger** | Flag for review when a rhetorical question mainly manufactures suspense, presupposes reader agreement, or delays a claim, especially in patterns such as “The question is…” followed by an immediately supplied obvious answer. Do not trigger on question form alone. |
| **7. Academic risk** | A formulaic setup can obscure the proposition being advanced, assume consensus, or introduce an unnecessarily promotional tone. |
| **8. Protected contexts** | Formally stated research questions; genuine unresolved conceptual questions; questions used to organise an inquiry; pedagogically necessary prompts; accurately quoted questions; questions whose alternatives are subsequently analysed. |
| **9. Applicable sections** | Literature Review, Discussion, and General Academic Writing. In Methodology and Findings, protect explicit research questions and analytic questions reproduced as part of the study design or reporting structure. |
| **10. Severity** | Low |
| **11. User-facing explanation** | This question may create suspense or presume agreement rather than state or investigate a substantive issue. |
| **12. Suggested action** | Check whether the question is genuinely investigated; if it is only a setup, consider stating the proposition directly. The user may reject the suggestion. |
| **13. Do-not-auto-rewrite constraint** | Do not convert questions into claims automatically, remove research questions, supply an answer, infer reader agreement, or rewrite the paragraph. |
| **14. Evidence status** | Unvalidated pilot heuristic. Rhetorical intent, question–answer distance, and disciplinary conventions require human review and corpus testing. |
| **15. Trigger-positive synthetic example** | **Synthetic example:** “What is the obvious conclusion? Clearly, only one view can be accepted.” |
| **16. Protected / false-positive synthetic example** | **Synthetic example:** “How should the construct be distinguished from its measurement? This conceptual question guides the comparison below.” |
